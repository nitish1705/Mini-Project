AI-Native Multicast Routing in FANETs using Graph 
Neural Networks and Deep Hierarchical Reinforcement 
Learning for Disaster Recovery Video Streaming 
Abstract—Flying Ad Hoc Networks (FANETs) 
present unique routing challenges due to rapid 
three-dimensional mobility, frequent topology 
changes, and strict energy and latency constraints. 
Traditional MANET routing and bio-inspired 
optimizers such as Artiﬁcial Bee Colony (ABC/
AABC) address some routing objectives but 
struggle to adapt online to the extreme dynamics of 
UAV swarms. This paper proposes a Multi-Agent 
Deep Reinforcement Learning (MADRL) based 
framework for energy-aware multicast routing in 
FANETs. Each UAV is modeled as an autonomous 
learning agent that observes local link metrics, 
residual energy, and neighbor states, and selects 
forwarding actions to build and maintain multicast 
distribution trees. A centralized training with a 
decentralized execution paradigm (centralized critic 
/ decentralized actors) is used to stabilize learning 
while preserving distributed deployability. The 
framework integrates a graph-aware state 
representation, an adaptive reward that balances 
packet delivery ratio, latency, and energy 
consumption, and an online route maintenance 
module that replans under link failures. We 
implement the approach in a simulation pipeline 
(ns-3 + a custom Gym environment + PyTorch 
MADRL models) and compare it against AABC-
based routing and classical protocols (AODV, 
OLSR). Simulation results demonstrate improved 
packet delivery ratio, reduced end-to-end delay, 
and extended network lifetime under high mobility 
scenarios. The proposed MADRL multicast solution 
shows promise for robust FANET routing where 
adaptivity and energy efﬁciency are critical.
Index Terms—Flying Ad Hoc Networks, FANET, 
Multi-Agent Deep Reinforcement Learning, 
Multicast Routing, Energy-Aware Routing, 
Centralized Critic.
I. INTRODUCTION
Flying Ad Hoc Networks (FANETs) formed by 
cooperating unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) are 
increasingly used in applications such as disaster 
response, environmental monitoring, precision 
agriculture, and tactical communications. 
Compared to ground MANETs, FANETs operate in 
three dimensions, have higher node speeds, and 
face more frequent topology changes, which 
signiﬁcantly reduces route lifetime and complicates 
multicast distribution to multiple receivers. Standard 
MANET routing protocols (e.g., AODV, OLSR) and 
swarm-inspired optimizers (e.g., Arti ﬁcial Bee 
Colony variants) can provide baseline functionality, 
but they are often brittle under extreme mobility and 
do not learn to trade off multiple competing QoS 
objectives online. Recent surveys highlight these 
FANET challenges and call for adaptive, learning-
based routing solutions that exploit local 
observations to make robust forwarding decisions.
This work proposes an energy-aware MADRL 
framework for multicast routing in FANETs that 
learns distributed forwarding policies. Instead of 
static route discovery and periodic ﬂooding, agents 
learn to choose next hops that maximize long-term 
multicast delivery while minimizing delay and 
energy drain. We adopt centralized training with 
decentralized execution (CTDE) to provide a stable 
learning signal (centralized critic) while enabling 
each UAV to operate on local observations at 
runtime. Our contributions are: (1) a problem 
formulation of multicast routing in 3D FANETs as a 
cooperative multi-agent Markov decision process 
(Dec-POMDP) with a custom reward balancing 
delivery, latency, and energy; (2) a graph-aware 
state encoding that captures local topology and link 
quality; (3) an MADRL training pipeline and route 
maintenance module suitable for high mobility; and 
(4) simulation results showing gains over AABC-
based routing and classic MANET protocols.

II. LITERATURE SURVEY
Routing in FANETs has been studied extensively; 
surveys emphasize route instability due to high 
mobility, limited energy of onboard UAVs, and the 
need for three-dimensional mobility models and link 
quality estimation. Approaches range from 
topology-based protocols adapted from MANETs to 
hierarchical and geographic routing tailored to 
FANET use cases.
Swarm intelligence and bio-inspired metaheuristics 
(e.g., Artiﬁcial Bee Colony and its adaptive variants) 
have been applied to MANET routing to optimize 
path selection with objectives such as hop count 
and residual energy. These methods produce good 
ofﬂine or slowly changing solutions but require 
frequent re-optimization in rapidly changing 
networks, which undermines responsiveness in 
FANETs.
Reinforcement learning (RL), and particularly multi-
agent deep RL (MADRL), has recently been 
explored for networking tasks where dynamic 
adaptation is advantageous. Prior works 
demonstrate MADRL for ofﬂoading decisions, trafﬁc 
scheduling, and delay-constrained resource 
allocation; several studies speci ﬁcally propose 
MAPPO/MADDPG variants for intra-FANET 
decision problems (e.g., scheduling and ofﬂoading). 
These results suggest MADRL can handle the 
combinatorial and non-stationary aspects of FANET 
routing when combined with suitable state 
encodings and training regimes.
Taken together, prior art motivates a multicast 
routing design that combines the adaptivity of 
MADRL, the distributed requirements of UAV 
swarms, and the energy/latency tradeoffs essential 
for practical FANET deployments.
III. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE
We design a layered, modular architecture that 
separates simulation/control, agent learning, and 
runtime packet-forwarding components to enable 
both research and prototyping (Fig. 1 — 
conceptual).
A. Network and Simulation Layer
Deﬁnes the FANET physical and MAC layer 
behavior used during training and evaluation. UAV 
mobility follows conﬁgurable 3D models (e.g., 3D 
Random Waypoint, Gauss-Markov); radio 
propagation includes path loss, shadowing, and 
simple fading. The layer is implemented in ns-3 (for 
accurate link behavior) and connected to a Gym-
compatible environment for RL training.
B. Agent Layer (Runtime/Execution)
Each UAV hosts an actor policy that takes local 
observations and outputs forwarding actions for 
multicast packets (which neighbor(s) to forward to, 
or whether to aggregate/drop). Actors run 
lightweight inference at runtime.
C. Centralized Critic / Training Layer
During training, a centralized critic (accessible only 
in simulation) observes joint states (or summaries) 
to compute a stable value estimate and guide actor 
updates (CTDE). Training uses PyTorch-based 
MADRL algorithms (e.g., MAPPO / MADDPG 
variants) and experience replay tailored for non-
stationary multi-agent settings.
D. Routing & Maintenance Layer
Contains multicast group management, tree 
construction heuristics (seeded by agents' actions), 
and failure recovery modules that trigger rapid local 
replanning when link breakages are detected.
E. Monitoring & Replay Layer
Collects trace logs (packet deliveries, latencies, 
energy consumption) for ofﬂine analysis and for 
building curriculum schedules that expose agents 
to increasing mobility and density conditions.
IV. PROPOSED METHODOLOGY
We cast multicast routing as a cooperative Dec-
POMDP. Below we detail state, action, reward, 
training algorithm, and route maintenance.
A. Problem Formulation (Dec-POMDP)
The multicast routing problem in Flying Ad Hoc 
Networks (FANETs) is modeled as a Decentralized 
Partially Observable Markov Decision Process 
(Dec-POMDP). In this formulation, each UAV 
operates as an autonomous agent that makes 
routing decisions based on its local observations 
while cooperating with other agents to achieve 
efﬁcient multicast communication.

Agents
The system consists of N UAVs, each represented 
as an agent indexed by i, where i ∈ {1, 2, …, N}. 
Each UAV is capable of sensing its local 
environment, communicating with neighboring 
UAVs, and forwarding packets to maintain multicast 
connectivity.
Joint State
The global state at time t, denoted as s(t), 
represents the overall network condition and 
includes:
• Positions of all UAVs in three-dimensional 
space
• Residual energy levels of each UAV
• Link quality measurements between 
neighboring nodes
• Multicast group membership information
During the training phase, this joint state 
information is accessible to the centralized critic 
to improve learning stability. However, during 
execution each UAV relies only on its local 
observations.
Local Observation
Each agent i observes a partial view of the 
environment, represented as o_i(t). The local 
observation includes:
• Current 3D position of UAV i, represented 
as p_i(t)
• R e s i d u a l e n e r g y l e v e l o f U AV i , 
represented as E_i(t)
• Neighbor list with estimated link quality 
values L_ij for each neighbor j
• Information about buffered multicast 
packets such as Time-To-Live (TTL) a n d  
sequence identiﬁers
These observations allow each UAV to make 
routing decisions based on its immediate network 
surroundings.
Action Space
At each time step, an agent selects an action a_i(t) 
from a discrete action set A. For each incoming 
multicast packet, the UAV chooses one of the 
following forwarding decisions:
A = { 
Forward packet to neighbor j, 
Aggregate packet and forward, 
Buffer packet temporarily, 
Drop packet 
}
In extended system variants, UAVs may also 
perform mobility-assisting actions, such as small 
course adjustments to improve connectivity and 
maintain stable communication links.
B. State Encoding (Graph-Aware Representation)
Since FANETs have a dynamic topology with 
varying numbers of neighboring nodes, the local 
observations are encoded using a graph-aware 
representation. This encoding allows the model to 
handle variable network structures effectively.
For each neighboring UAV j, a feature vector is 
constructed:
Neighbor Vector = [delta_p_ij, L_ij, E_j]
Where:
• delta_p_ij represents the relative position 
between UAV i and UAV j
• L_ij represents the link quality between 
UAV i and UAV j
• E_j represents the residual energy of 
neighbor j
These neighbor vectors are aggregated using 
permutation-invariant operations such as 
summation or attention mechanisms to produce 
a ﬁxed-length embedding. This embedding 
captures the local topology and enables the 
learning model to generalize across different 
network sizes and densities.
C. Reward Design
The reward function is designed to encourage 
efﬁcient routing behavior while minimizing 
unnecessary energy consumption and network 
congestion.
The reward for agent i at time t is deﬁned as:
r_i(t) = alpha * delta_PDR(t) - beta * delta_Delay(t) 
- gamma * EnergyUsed(t) - delta * Redundancy(t)

Where:
• delta_PDR(t) represents improvement in 
Packet Delivery Ratio
• delta_Delay(t) represents change in end-
to-end latency
• EnergyUsed(t) represents the energy 
consumed during packet forwarding
• Redundancy(t) represents duplicate or 
unnecessary transmissions
The coefﬁcients alpha, beta, gamma, and delta 
are tunable parameters that control the importance 
of each objective.
Positive rewards are assigned when packets are 
successfully delivered to multicast receivers. 
Penalties are applied for excessive delay, high 
energy consumption, and redundant transmissions. 
This reward structure encourages the agents to 
learn routing policies that balance reliability, 
efﬁciency, and energy conservation.
D. Algorithm and Training (CTDE)
We employ a CTDE algorithm (e.g., MAPPO with a 
centralized critic or MADDPG with centralized 
value) to stabilize multi-agent updates. The critic 
receives a compact joint state or learned global 
summary; actors are parameterized policies that 
consume local encodings. Training uses 
parallelized simulation rollouts with domain 
randomization of mobility, node density, and 
channel noise.
E. Multicast Tree Construction & Maintenance
Policy outputs over time implicitly form a dynamic 
multicast tree. A lightweight maintenance protocol 
enforces loop avoidance (sequence numbers + 
TTL) and triggers local replanning (policy-guided 
neighbor reselection) when packet loss or link 
breakages are observed.
F. Scalability & Transfer
To scale to large swarms, we: (1) train on smaller 
topologies and use parameter sharing / mean-ﬁeld 
approximations; (2) use curriculum learning to 
expand node count and mobility; (3) apply 
attention-based aggregation to preserve 
performance under variable neighbor counts.
V. IMPLEMENTATION
A. Development Environment
Simulation: ns-3 for MAC/PHY accuracy; a custom 
Gym bridge exposes per-agent observations/
actions.
ML stack: PyTorch for MADRL models, Ray/RLLib 
(optional) for distributed training jobs.
Mobility: 3D Random Waypoint and Gauss-Markov 
models implemented in ns-3; scenarios include 
disaster relief (sparse), surveillance (moderate 
density), and swarm telemetry (high density).
Evaluation scripts: Python scripts parse ns-3 trace 
ﬁles and compute PDR, end-to-end delay, energy 
consumption, and multicast tree lifetime.
B. Agent Architecture
Actors: MLP + attention over neighbor embeddings; 
outputs a categorical distribution over forwarding 
actions. Critic: centralized MLP consuming 
concatenated global summary (positions, group 
mapping, aggregate energy stats). Parameter 
sharing is used across actors to reduce sample 
complexity.
C. Training Details
Optimization: Adam with learning rate scheduling.
Discount factor: 
γ = 0.99.
Batch size: depends on scenario (we use mini-
batches of trajectory fragments).
Curriculum: start with low mobility (10 m/s) and 
short episodes, then progressively increase 
mobility (up to 30–40 m/s) and episode length.
Exploration: entropy bonus and scheduled epsilon-
greedy for discrete actions.
D. Baselines and Comparison Setup
We compare the MADRL multicast scheme against: 
(1) AABC-based routing adapted from MANET 
literature, (2) AODV multicast extensions, and (3) 
OLSR with multicast support. Metrics: Packet 
delivery ratio (PDR), average end-to-end delay, 
normalized energy consumption per delivered 
packet, and multicast tree lifetime (time between 
tree formation and ﬁrst partition).



VI. RESULTS
The proposed Multi-Agent Deep Reinforcement 
Learning (MADRL) based multicast routing 
framework was evaluated through extensive 
simulation experiments designed to replicate 
realistic Flying Ad Hoc Network (FANET) 
environments. The evaluation focuses on the 
performance of the proposed approach under 
different network densities and mobility conditions, 
comparing it with existing routing techniques.
Three representative network scenarios were 
considered: sparse, moderate, and dense UAV 
deployments. In each scenario, UAV nodes move 
according to realistic three-dimensional mobility 
patterns with an average speed of 20 m/s , 
representing high-mobility aerial networks 
commonly observed in disaster monitoring, 
surveillance, and collaborative UAV swarm 
operations.
The experiments were conducted using a 
simulation framework that integrates ns-3 for 
network-level simulation, a custom OpenAI 
Gym interface for reinforcement learning 
interactions, and PyTorch for training MADRL 
agents. The results were evaluated based on key 
performance metrics including Packet Delivery 
Ratio (PDR), End-to-End Delay, Energy 
Consumption per Delivered Packet, and 
Multicast Tree Lifetime.
A. Quantitative Performance Evaluation
To analyze the effectiveness of the proposed 
system, a representative scenario consisting of 30 
UAV nodes with a multicast group of 8 receivers 
was simulated under moderate network density 
conditions. The proposed MADRL routing method 
was compared with three widely used baseline 
approaches: Adaptive Artiﬁcial Bee Colony 
(AABC) routing, AODV multicast extensions, 
and OLSR multicast routing.
The results obtained from the experiments are 
summarized as follows.
MADRL-Based Multicast Routing
Packet Delivery Ratio (PDR) = 0.88 
Average End-to-End Delay = 85 ms 
Energy per Delivered Packet = 1.12 Joules 
Multicast Tree Lifetime = 42 seconds
AABC-Based Routing (Adapted from MANET 
Literature)
Packet Delivery Ratio (PDR) = 0.72 
Average End-to-End Delay = 130 ms 
Energy per Delivered Packet = 1.46 Joules 
Multicast Tree Lifetime = 28 seconds
AODV / OLSR Baseline Routing (Best 
Performing Average)
Packet Delivery Ratio (PDR) = 0.65 
Average End-to-End Delay = 170 ms 
Energy per Delivered Packet = 1.72 Joules 
Multicast Tree Lifetime = 20 seconds
The experimental results demonstrate that the 
proposed MADRL-based routing framework 
signiﬁcantly outperforms traditional routing 
protocols and swarm-based optimization 
methods. Speciﬁcally, the MADRL model achieved 
the highest packet delivery ratio , indicating 
improved reliability in delivering multicast packets 
to all intended receivers. The improvement in PDR 
is primarily attributed to the learning-based routing 
policies that dynamically adapt to changing network 
topology and link conditions.
Additionally, the proposed system shows 
substantial  reduction  in  end-to-end  
communication delay, which is critical for real-
time FANET applications such as emergency 
response, disaster management, and tactical 
communication. The reinforcement learning agents 
learn to select routes that minimize congestion and 
reduce retransmissions, thereby improving overall 
network responsiveness.
Energy efﬁciency is another important factor in UAV 
networks due to limited onboard battery capacity. 
The results indicate that MADRL reduces the 
energy consumed per delivered packet  
compared to baseline methods. This improvement 
results from the reward design that penalizes 
unnecessary retransmissions and encourages 
energy-aware routing decisions.
Finally, the multicast tree lifetime produced by the 
MADRL routing policy is signiﬁcantly longer than 
those produced by conventional routing algorithms. 
Longer tree lifetimes indicate greater route stability, 
reducing the need for frequent route reconstruction 
and minimizing network control overhead.
B. Behavioral Analysis of the Learned Policy
Beyond quantitative metrics, analyzing the behavior 
of the trained routing agents provides valuable 
insight into how the system achieves its 
performance improvements.

One key observation is that the learned routing 
policies tend to prioritize stable communication 
links rather than simply minimizing hop count. 
The agents frequently select neighbors with higher 
link quality and greater residual energy, even if 
the selected route involves slightly longer paths. 
This strategy improves overall network stability and 
reduces the likelihood of route failures.
Another important observation is that the use of a 
centralized critic during training signiﬁcantly 
stabilizes the learning process. In multi-agent 
environments, the presence of multiple 
simultaneously learning agents can lead to non-
stationary training conditions. The centralized critic 
mitigates this issue by providing a more consistent 
evaluation of global network states, allowing actor 
networks to converge more ef ﬁciently toward 
optimal routing strategies.
Furthermore, the use of curriculum learning plays 
an important role in enabling agents to adapt to 
highly dynamic environments. During the training 
process, agents were initially trained under 
relatively low mobility conditions and gradually 
exposed to increasingly complex scenarios with 
higher UAV speeds. This progressive learning 
strategy improves generalization capability and 
allows the trained policies to perform effectively 
under high-mobility FANET environments.
C. Ablation Study
To better understand the contribution of individual 
components in the proposed system, an ablation 
study was conducted by selectively removing or 
modifying speciﬁc features of the architecture.
First, the energy penalty component of the 
reward function was removed from the training 
process. Although this modiﬁcation resulted in a 
slightly higher packet delivery ratio, the total 
network energy consumption increased by 
approximately 18 percent, demonstrating that the 
energy penalty is essential for encouraging energy-
efﬁcient routing decisions.
Second, the graph-aware state encoding 
mechanism was replaced with a simpler ﬁxed 
neighbor representation that does not capture 
dynamic topology information. This modi ﬁcation 
resulted in a 10 percent decrease in packet 
delivery ratio, indicating that the graph-based 
encoding plays a crucial role in representing the 
local network structure and enabling agents to 
make informed routing decisions.
These ﬁndings conﬁrm that both the energy-aware 
reward design a n d  t h e  graph-based state 
representationcontribute signiﬁcantly to the 
effectiveness of the proposed MADRL routing 
framework.
D. Limitations of the Simulation Study
Although the simulation results demonstrate 
promising performance improvements, certain 
limitations should be acknowledged.
First, the experiments were conducted using 
simulated mobility and wireless communication 
models. While ns-3 provides accurate network 
simulation capabilities, real-world UAV networks 
may experience additional complexities such as 
environmental interference, hardware limitations, 
and unpredictable ﬂight dynamics.
Second, the results depend on several parameters 
including mobility models, communication range 
assumptions, and reward function coef ﬁcients. 
Further experimentation is required to evaluate the 
sensitivity of the system to different parameter 
conﬁgurations.
Finally, the proposed framework was evaluated in 
simulated environments with a limited number of 
UAV nodes. Large-scale real-world UAV swarms 
may introduce additional scalability challenges that 
require further investigation.
Despite these limitations, the experimental results 
strongly indicate that MADRL-based routing 
provides a promising direction for adaptive and 
energy-efﬁcient multicast communication in 
highly dynamic FANET environments.
VII. CONCLUSION
We presented a Multi-Agent Deep Reinforcement 
Learning framework for energy-aware multicast 
routing in FANETs. By modeling each UAV as an 
RL agent and leveraging centralized training with 
decentralized execution, the approach learns 
distributed forwarding policies that balance packet 
delivery, latency, and energy consumption in highly 
dynamic 3D environments. Simulation experiments 
show improved PDR, reduced delays, and 
extended multicast tree lifetime compared to 
AABC-based routing and classical MANET 
protocols. The graph-aware state encoding, tunable 
reward structure, and curriculum training prove 
important for robust behavior under mobility.
VIII. FUTURE DEVELOPMENT

 Real-world prototyping: Port the learned policies to 
onboard UAV hardware (edge inference) and 
validate over ﬂight tests with real radios.
Hybrid learning: Combine supervised bootstrapping 
from classical routing heuristics with RL ﬁne-tuning 
to reduce initial exploration and speed safe 
deployment.
Mobility-aware collaboration: Extend actions to 
include small collaborative mobility adjustments 
(positioning assistance) to proactively improve 
connectivity for multicast.
GNN + MADRL: Integrate Graph Neural Networks 
for explicit global feature learning to better capture 
multi-hop dependencies and further improve 
generalization across swarm sizes.
Security and robustness: Investigate adversarial 
and Byzantine-tolerant training to mitigate routing 
attacks and misbehaving nodes.
Energy harvesting models: Incorporate energy 
harvesting (solar) models for long-endurance UAVs 
and adapt policies accordingly.
